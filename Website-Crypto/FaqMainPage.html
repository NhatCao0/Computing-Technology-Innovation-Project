Frequently Asked Questions





Cryptocurrency is a digital or virtual form of currency that uses cryptography for security and operates independently of a central authority, such as a government or bank.


Cryptocurrencies work on a decentralized technology called blockchain, which is a distributed ledger that records all transactions across a network of computers. Transactions are verified by network nodes through cryptography and recorded in blocks that are linked together, forming a chain.


Cryptocurrency can be acquired through various means, including buying on cryptocurrency exchanges, mining (the process of validating transactions and adding them to the blockchain), receiving it as payment for goods or services, or through initial coin offerings (ICOs) and token sales.


The value of cryptocurrencies can be highly volatile, and investing in them carries risks. Some people have made significant profits from investing in cryptocurrencies, while others have experienced losses. It's essential to conduct thorough research and consider your risk tolerance before investing in cryptocurrencies.


The legal status of cryptocurrencies varies from country to country. While some countries have embraced cryptocurrencies and enacted regulations to govern their use, others have banned or restricted their use. It's essential to be aware of the legal framework surrounding cryptocurrencies in your jurisdiction.


Cryptocurrency mining consumes a significant amount of energy, primarily due to the computational power required to solve complex cryptographic puzzles. Critics argue that the energy consumption associated with mining, particularly for proof-of-work cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin, contributes to environmental degradation and carbon emissions. Efforts are underway to develop more energy-efficient consensus mechanisms and promote sustainable mining practices in the crypto industry.
